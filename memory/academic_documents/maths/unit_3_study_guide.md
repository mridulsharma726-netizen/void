# Unit 3: Probability and Statistics in Mathematics

## Chapter 1: Basic Concepts

### 1.1 Probability
**Definition:** A probability is a measure of how likely an event will occur.

- **Example:** Rolling a fair six-sided die, where each side has an equal chance of landing face up.
  
### 1.2 Conditional Probability
**Definition:** The probability of one event occurring given that another event has already occurred.

- **Example:** If you flip a coin and it lands on heads, the probability of getting heads is 0.5 (or 50%).

### 1.3 Bayes' Theorem
**Definition:** A theorem in probability theory that allows us to update our beliefs based on new evidence.

- **Formula:** P(A|B) = [P(B|A) * P(A)] / P(B)
  
  - **Example:** If we know that the weather is sunny, what's the probability it will rain tomorrow? We can use Bayes' Theorem to find this out: P(Rain|Sunny) = [P(Sunny|Rain) * P(Rain | Sunny)] / P(Sunny)

## Chapter 2: Random Variables

### 2.1 Random Variables
**Definition:** A random variable is a measurable function from the sample space of an experiment to the real numbers.

- **Example:** Rolling a fair six-sided die, where each roll can be represented by a number between 1 and 6.
  
### 2.2 Cumulative Distribution Functions (CDF)
**Definition:** The CDF of a random variable is the probability that the random variable takes on values less than or equal to a given value.

- **Example:** If we have a random variable X, its CDF is defined as F(x) = P(X ≤ x).
  
### 2.3 Probability Distributions
**Definition:** A distribution of a random variable is a collection of all possible outcomes and their corresponding probabilities.

- **Examples:** The normal distribution, which has a bell-shaped curve with mean μ and standard deviation σ.
  
## Chapter 3: Probability Distributions

### 3.1 Discrete Distributions
**Definition:** A discrete probability distribution is one where the random variable can take on only certain values.

- **Examples:** Binomial distribution, Poisson distribution, geometric distribution.
  
### 3.2 Continuous Distributions
**Definition:** A continuous probability distribution is one where the random variable can take any value within a given range.

- **Examples:** Normal distribution, uniform distribution, exponential distribution.
  
### 3.3 Moments of Distributions
**Definition:** The mean (expected value) and variance are moments of a distribution.

- **Example:** For a normal distribution with mean μ and standard deviation σ, the mean is μ and the variance is σ².

## Chapter 4: Expected Value

**Definition:** The expected value of a random variable is the long-run average value of repetitions of the experiment it represents.

- **Formula:** E(X) = ∑(x * P(x))
  
  - **Example:** If you roll a fair six-sided die, the expected value is (1 + 2 + 3 + 4 + 5 + 6) / 6 = 3.5.
  
## Chapter 5: Variance and Standard Deviation

**Definition:** The variance of a random variable is a measure of how spread out the values are.

- **Formula:** Var(X) = E[(X - E(X))^2]
  
  - **Example:** If you roll a fair six-sided die, the variance is (1 + 4 + 9 + 6)^2 / 36 ≈ 0.75.
  
## Chapter 6: Central Limit Theorem

**Definition:** A theorem in probability theory that states that the sum of a large number of independent and identically distributed random variables will be approximately normally distributed.

- **Example:** If you roll a fair six-sided die many times, the distribution of the sample mean will approximate a normal distribution with mean 3.5 and standard deviation 1.
  
## Chapter 7: Normal Distribution

**Definition:** A normal distribution is a continuous probability distribution that is symmetric about its mean.

- **Example:** The heights of adult males in the United States are normally distributed with a mean of 68 inches and a standard deviation of 2.5 inches.
